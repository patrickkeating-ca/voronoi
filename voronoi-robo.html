Fintech & Geometry — No. 01

Every client belongs to whichever model portfolio is closest.

A robo-advisor assigns each client to the nearest of a handful of fixed model portfolios in risk/horizon space. Draw that assignment rule as a Voronoi tessellation and the boundaries stop being invisible — you can see exactly which clients sit on a fence between two portfolios, one small answer away from being reclassified. Mock data, 320 synthetic clients, 6 model portfolios — but the regime overlay below is real: it reads today's actual classification from the companion market-regime map.

The cell is the client segment

Six seed points — the model portfolios — partition the whole plane into regions of nearest neighbor. A new prospect's risk score and horizon land somewhere on the map, and whichever cell contains that point is the portfolio they're assigned. The cell's shape isn't designed, it falls out of the seeds' relative positions.

Boundaries are contestable clients

Dots ringed in white sit nearly equidistant between two seeds — small movements in a risk questionnaire or a horizon update would flip their assignment. That's the client base most exposed to model changes, repricing, or a competitor drawing its seeds slightly differently.

The overlay moves the seeds, not the clients

Switching the regime chip shifts every model portfolio's risk coordinate at once — horizon stays put, since that's a client fact, not a market one. No client answers a new question, yet some get reclassified purely because the market moved. The count of who shifted, versus a neutral baseline, is in the ledger below.
